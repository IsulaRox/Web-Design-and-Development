Reduce Ocean Acidification

Ocean acidification can be defined as the reduction in the pH of the ocean during a certain period of time due to the dissolving and absorption of carbon dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere. This is a major concern in the worldwide marine ecosystems. Ocean lives struggle to survive and to maintain the standards in acidic environments.

Causes

  • The Industrial Revolution
  • The use of greenhouse gas emitting fossil fuels.
  • Deforestation

    • Due to these main key factors the composition of atmospheric carbon dioxide has been rapidly increased. This has been upsetting the Earth's carbon cycle and distorted its natural balance. All the oceans in the world absorb nearly 30% of the carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. However, when there is excess carbon dioxide in the atmosphere that directly causes damages to the chemical structure of the seawater. Carbon dioxide dissolution in the seawater in large quantities, very unhealthy chemical reactions can happen. That would cause the increase of hydrogen ions and the seawater becomes very acidic and levels of carbonate ions can be diminishing.

    From NOAA

    Effects

    As we said above, lower levels of carbonate ions due to the ocean acidification can have effect on

  • marine organisms such as coral and plankton to form shells and skeletons.
  • even cause some existing shells to dissolve
  • corrodes existing coral structure
  • slows the development of new skeletons
  • form weaker coral structures that are more vulnerable to damage
  • the shells of mussels and oysters will be decreased by around 25% and 10% respectively. these creatures are more at risk of being crushed or eaten (Anderson, 2023).
  • Oyster larvae are also affected - they need to form a shell to start feeding, lack of carbonate in the water is preventing them from doing so (Anderson, 2023).
  • Plankton shells quickly dissolve.
  • some species dying out, and others migrating regions
  • life-threatening, or cause respiratory and gastrointestinal illness in humans.
  • This increased PH balance can hugely affect the physiology of the fish, resulting in increased metabolic rates and behavioural changes (Anderson, 2023).

    • Solutions

  • Microalgae cultivation and carbon reduction
  • Seaweed growth for carbon sequestration

    • The potential is huge. Plants in the ocean, from seagrasses to plankton, add up to just 0.05 percent of the plant biomass on land, but are so pervasive and efficient at sucking up carbon that they cycle through roughly the same amount of carbon every day as all the land-based plants. Yet seagrass ecosystems are being wiped out, thanks to everything from pandemic disease to water pollution and coastal construction projects. The rate of loss has skyrocketed from less than 1 percent of global seagrass cover per year in the 1970s to 7 percent annually in the 2000s, making seagrasses one of the planet’s most threatened ecosystems. Efforts to restore or farm such plants could have a host of benefits, including soaking up atmospheric carbon (Jones, 2016).

    Kelp is also well known for soaking up excess nutrients and making waters cleaner for shellfish. Most academic papers looking at the benefits of kelp don’t even mention acidification. But it didn’t take much for Nichole Price of the Bigelow Laboratory for Ocean Science in Maine to put two and two together. “The biggest challenge in land-based kelp nurseries is keeping the pH low enough because they consume so much CO2,” says Price, who wondered how those same photosynthetic algae were affecting ocean waters (Jones, 2016).

  • Ocean alkalinity enhancement
  • direct air capture plants on land, using electrochemistry to essentially strip CO₂ from seawater. The acidic stream of CO₂ can be stored in deep rock layers or used to weather
  • Blue carbon ecosystems. Coastal ecosystems, including tidal salt marshes, mangrove forests, and seagrass meadows

    • Conclusion

    Positive news is making waves in the waves! Perhaps the answer to healing climate change lies in the ocean, which has been hit hardest. We can all work together to make the future of our world more peaceful by encouraging the ocean to store more carbon and looking into benign ways to remove it.
